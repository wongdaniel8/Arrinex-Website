Understanding Rhinitis...



How common is it?

It's common!

About 60-80 million Americans have either allergic or non-allergic rhinitis. The difference? One is triggered by allergies, the other is triggered by unknown causes.



How annoying is it?

It's annoying!

Both allergic and non-allergic rhinitis result in allergy symptoms such as a runny nose, itchy eyes and skin, sneezing, stuffy nose, and fatigue.



How easily is it triggered?

Easily!

Common triggers include pollen, grass, pet hair, and dust mites which is pretty close. Non-allergenic triggers include cleaning solutions, cigarette smoke, hair spray, and car exhaust.

  • Nasal Rinsing

    is a process that involves squirting a saline solution up the nostrils to purge the nasal cavities. Nasal rinses can be repulsive and unfomfortable. At best, nasal rinses only bring about temporary relief from certain allergy symptoms.

  • Medication

    is often used to mask the symptoms of rhinitis by temporarily suppressing the release of histamines within the body. Even if medication is successful in reducing the irritating affects of rhinitis, they must be constantly replenished, which can take a financial toll.

  • Steriod Nasal Sprays

    work by inhaling a liquid-aerosol medication up your nose for the purpose of reducing inflammation of the nasal lining. Depending on the type, some nasal sprays require several days to a week of continued use before any improvement in symptoms can be noticed.

  • Sinus Surgery

    is used in certain cases of chronic rhinits, but is often seen as a last resort. Surgery can help drain fluid built up in the sinuses or remove nasal polyps, either of which could be the result of allergic or nonallergic rhinitis.

  • Dealing With It

    When the prospect of continually seeking medication or constantly trying different therapies seems to have no avail, many people are left with the unfortunate choice of seeking temporary relief, or just living with chronic rhinitis.
